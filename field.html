Field × work

My fieldwork focuses on languages of the Himalayas, particularly Bodish languages spoken in China and Nepal. I have worked both in situ and with diasporic communities, and, more recently, in remote-work settings. In my work I espouse a community-engagement philosophy.

Tibetan

"Tibetan" is an umbrella term for the Tibetic languages, a branch of the Bodish subgroup in the Sino-Tibetan family. Tibetic encompasses all modern descendants of the Old Tibetan language, a later version of which is attested as early as the 7th Century, in the Dunhuang Manuscripts. Modern Tibetic languages are known for their intricate evidential and epistemic marking systems.

I work primarily on Lhasa Tibetan, a Central Tibetic (Ü-Tsang) variety spoken in the city of Lhasa and, in a variant form, in diasporic communities.

My work started out as collaborative entrepreneurial endeavor with a Lhasa-based educational start-up, and I continue to partner with them in course development and the promotion of language use and learning.

Gurung

Gurung (Tamu Kyi, Nepali गुरुङ भाषा) is a minority language spoken in Gandaki Pradesh, central Nepal. The Gurung people are sometimes known for their service as Gurkha soldiers, and for a fascinating honey-hunting practice.

A member of the Tamangic branch under the Bodish subgroup of Sino-Tibetan and moderately related to Tibetan, the Gurung language does not have a written tradition. Nepali is used in all educational, formal and non-local communication settings.

I have worked with members of the Gurung community in New York City and in the village of Sikles (Ćiili naasa), Kaski District, Nepal, in collaboration with the Endangered Language Alliance and colleagues Zach Wellstood and Danielle Ronkos.
