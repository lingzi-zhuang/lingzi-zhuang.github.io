Research

My research program brings together formal theories/tools and diachronically and typologically grounded understanding of languages.

Topics I work on include mirativity, evidentiality, attitude expressions, event structure and nominal reference, and definiteness. I am especially interested in cross-linguistic generalizations and diachrony in these domains.

I also consider how formally precise analyses can shed light on the linguistic history of Sino-Tibetan and its subgroups.

Projects

Mirativity

My ongoing dissertation project investigates the notional category of mirativity (grammaticalized markers of new, unexpected or surprising information). The goal is to develop a cross-linguistically general theory that captures the core semantic and pragmatic nature of this meaning domain.

I draw on data collected from ongoing fieldwork on understudied Sino-Tibetan languages (e.g. Tibetan, Wu), funded through various grants including, most recently, an award from the Engaged Cornell Serve In Place Fund and the year-long Hu Shih Fellowship in Chinese Studies.

Definiteness in bare nominals

Bare nominals in Mandarin can be uniqueness definites quite freely - except in the post-verbal position where definiteness is strongly restricted. I investigate these restrictions systematically.

Reconstructing Bodish

Segmental reconstruction has been a major challenge in Sino-Tibetan historical linguistics, due to overwhelming monosyllabicity of etyma and widespread segment-eroding tonogenesis. I show for the Bodish branch that certain morphological elements can indeed be reconstructed if one follows a principled analysis of lexicalization and semantic shift.

I use a combination of textual, lexicographical and fieldwork data spanning multiple subgroups (Tibetic, Tamangic, Eastern Bodish).
